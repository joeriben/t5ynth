T5ynth

Resonance with meaning.

T5ynth opens hidden spaces of possible sound. Two short impulses mark the poles of a space of meaning you define. The synth lets you explore what can become audible between them. Set those poles as textures, transients, patterns, sonic and musical fragments, field recordings, everyday noises, orchestral gestures, alien voices, human emotional expressions, or impossible hybrids.

Impulse A and Impulse B are not two samples, two song requests, or two oscillators. They are two markers for the space you want to explore. You can pull between the markers, push the space harder or softer, disturb it, bend it along sound qualities, open individual dimensions, change where one idea enters the other, and let the space drift over time while it remains open as a space of possibilities.

Most AI audio tools hide that space and only return an audio result. T5ynth turns this space of possible sound into the instrument. Generation is not a separate AI step after the synth; the synth reaches into the generation process itself. The rendered fragment is one stage in a signal path that continues through sampler or wavetable playback, filters, envelopes, LFOs, sequencing, delay, and reverb.

Before You Start: What T5ynth Is

If you come from synthesizers, the clearest mental model is simple: T5ynth begins before the oscillator has become audio. On another synth you might select "saw", load a sample, or scan a wavetable. In T5ynth you enter short impulses such as "steady clean saw wave, C3" and "120 bpm syncopated transient pattern". Those impulses mark a hidden sound space inside the selected audio model.

You do not need to know what "T5" is to use the instrument. In practice, T5 is the text-reading part inside the model: it turns words into numbers the audio model can respond to. T5ynth opens up musical control over the hidden space where the model decides what sound can become. You are not operating a black box and waiting for a result; you are exploring the model's space of possibilities.

The interface calls the two text fields Impulse A and Impulse B. In musical use, read them as two meaning poles: two markers for one inner sound space, not two finished audio files, not two song requests, and not two oscillators. Neither pole is an attachment to the other: A and B define the relation that opens the space. Alpha does not mix two rendered samples; it moves through the space of possibilities from which one fragment can become audible.

After the generated fragment enters the playback engine, the tools become more familiar: playback mode, pitch, filter, envelopes, modulation, sequencers, and effects. Leaving a text field empty is an aesthetic choice which T5ynth will interpret as an echo chamber for the present impulse. There is no such thing as "no meaning" in creative processes.

The signal flow is therefore different from both ordinary synths and ordinary text-to-audio tools:

  1. Two short impulses mark the hidden space.
  2. Alpha, Semantic Axes, Noise, Dimension Explorer, Injection Modes, and Drift move that space of possibilities.
  3. Shape the sonic flow as a Sampler or Wavetable engine, with filters, envelopes, LFOs, sequencers, delay, reverb, presets, and Drift Modulators & Auto-Regenerate.
Preset starting points: T5ynth ships with presets, and an expanded collection lives at https://github.com/joeriben/T5ynth-Presets. A preset is a complete instrument state: impulses, generated audio, Sampler or Wavetable mode, modulation, effects, tags, and stored settings.

Device Selection

T5ynth auto-detects the best available compute device:

  • Apple Silicon Mac → MPS (Metal Performance Shaders), ~3× faster than CPU
  • NVIDIA GPU on Linux or Windows 11 → CUDA
  • No GPU → CPU fallback (works, but slow)

You can override the device in the generation section. The device toggle switches between GPU and CPU.

First Generation

On first launch, the status bar shows backend connection status. Once you see "Ready", enter a sound marker in Impulse A and click Generate. The first generation takes longer because the model is loaded into GPU memory on demand. Subsequent generations are faster.

If the status bar shows an error, check: (1) Is at least one model downloaded? (2) Is there enough disk space and RAM/VRAM? Stable Audio Open 1.0 needs ~4 GB VRAM.
Part I

1. The Generation Section (Possible Sound Space)

T5ynth's Generation section is where the instrument opens a space of possible sound. Instead of choosing a sine, saw, sample, or wavetable, you mark a bounded hidden sound space inside the selected audio model. In that sense, the T5ynth Oscillator is a meta-oscillator: it does not start from one waveform, but from a space of possible sounds. The main purpose is to explore that space, shape the generation itself, and decide which state becomes a short playable fragment.

The Two Text Fields: A & B

You have two text input fields. The UI labels them Impulse A and Impulse B. They are not two conventional oscillators, not two rendered samples, not two tracks, and not two ChatGPT-style commands. They are two markers for one inner sound space.

A and B are equal markers for one hidden sound space. Each impulse is turned into internal control data by the model's text-reading stage. That control data is not audio; it is the model's way of deciding what kind of sound can become. T5ynth changes that internal state so different fragments can become audible.

T5ynth can use Stable Audio Open 1.0, Stable Audio Open Small, or AudioLDM2. Each engine opens a different learned space. The Stable Audio engines are strongest with English sound-oriented phrases: sound effects, field recordings, drum or instrument loops, ambient sounds, foley, and production elements. AudioLDM2 can also cover sound effects, speech-like material, and music, but it is non-commercial only and behaves differently from the Stable Audio engines.

The impulses do not have to be narrow "sound ideas". They can evoke sound sources, materials in motion, acoustic scenes, bodies, gestures, moods, fictional agents, or impossible combinations. A visual phrase such as "a rose in a vase" is not a sound by itself; "water in a glass vase, quiet room, petals brushed by fingers" gives the model acoustic handles. But the abstract phrase can still be used as a strange marker in the model's space. T5ynth is where you find out what that marker can become.

A and B mark a space of possible sound. In the interface, Alpha is the pull between A and B, Magnitude is how strongly the space is pushed, Noise perturbs it, Semantic Axes bend it along sound qualities, the Dimension Explorer opens individual dimensions, Injection Modes decide where B enters A during generation, and Drift moves the space over time.
  • Two marked poles — Impulse A and Impulse B are equal markers for one hidden sound space. The Alpha slider (labeled A ↔ B) moves through that space and changes what can become audible when the active Injection Mode is Linear; in other modes the slider's role shifts (see the mode-row explanation below).
  • One field empty: Echoraum — leaving either text field empty is an aesthetic choice. T5ynth interprets the empty side as an echo chamber for the present impulse: a reflection through the model's neutral point. Alpha then traces a path from the impulse through that neutral midpoint to the impulse's tonal antipode — sounds the impulse actively isn't. There is no such thing as "no meaning" in creative processes.
  • Both fields empty — both sides use the model's neutral point. The model produces its own self-portrait (pure unconditional sampling), varying with the seed.
Impulses should name acoustic qualities, actions, and situations, not musical genres. "A warm resonant drone with metallic harmonics" works better than "ambient techno." The model has no reliable concept of genre.
Leaving a field empty is an active musical decision, not a fallback. If you want the same character regardless of the A ↔ B slider, type the same text in both fields — that is mathematically equivalent to a pinned single-impulse sound (since 0.5·A + 0.5·A = A for every value of Alpha).

Injection Modes (research)

Above the Alpha slider sits a row of six small buttons — Linear · Step-in · Layer · Combo 1 · Combo 2 · Combo 3. These select different ways Impulse B enters Impulse A inside the diffusion pipeline. The classical A↔B movement is one of six options — you can think of it as the question "where in the generation process should B enter?". Different answers produce audibly different sounds from the same impulses and seed.

Clicking a mode button immediately triggers a fresh generation, and each mode keeps its own slider position so A/B-ing the modes by clicking buttons does not disturb the others. Only Stable Audio Open Small implements the non-linear modes; on other models the buttons fall through to Linear behaviour.

ModeWhat it doesSlider role
Linear The classical A/B movement through the hidden sound space (and beyond, via extrapolation). Bit-identical to T5ynth v1.5.x. This is the safe, well-understood default. α ∈ [−1, +1], A↔B with extrapolation past the poles.
Step-in Early sampler steps see Impulse A only; after a transition step the cross-attention conditioning swaps to a Step-in-controlled blend of A and B. The result has the broad shape of A and the refinement / texture of B (or a controlled mix). This mode lets B colour the late detail of A without dominating its overall envelope. Intensity 0–1: 0 = barely audible late blend, 1 = early transition + pure B in the late phase.
Layer The 16 cross-attention layers of the diffusion transformer are split into a B-zone defined by a two-thumb range slider. Layers inside the zone see Impulse B; layers outside see Impulse A. Different layer ranges produce qualitatively different mixes (low layers → surface texture, mid layers → structural conditioning). Two-thumb range slider 0–16, defining the B-zone start and end.
Combo 1 Step phase × layer band, preset to "B as surface skin": B injected only into the lowest 4 cross-attention layers, only in the late sampling phase. A relatively subtle variation — the bulk of the sound is still A, but the surface character is coloured by B. Intensity 0–1, Step-in-style coupled mapping.
Combo 2 Step phase × layer band, preset to "broad mid": B injected into 8 mid-range layers (4..11) in the late phase. The most distinct of the three Combos in empirical tests — the mid layers carry most of the diffusion's conditioning work, so a strong B presence there reshapes the result substantially. Intensity 0–1, Step-in-style coupled mapping.
Combo 3 Step phase × layer band, preset to "narrow center": B injected into 4 high-leverage center layers (6..9) in the late phase. Same width as Combo 1 but a different vertical position, probing whether where in the layer stack matters as much as how wide. Intensity 0–1, Step-in-style coupled mapping.
Drift LFOs targeting Alpha automatically modulate the active mode's intensity axis: in Linear it modulates α, in Step-in and the Combos it modulates the intensity slider, in Layer it slides the band along its width. The drift system stays mode-aware without any extra UI.

2. Sound-Character Axes

Below the A/B area, three dropdown slots let you apply sound-character axes — continuous sliders between two conceptual poles. Each axis nudges the current possible-sound space. In the UI and technical background these are also called semantic axes; musically, treat them as broad tone-character directions, not as precise EQ controls.

3. Dimension Explorer

The Dimension Explorer gives you direct access to all 768 internal control dimensions that steer the model. While the A/B text fields and sound-character axes are readable handles, the DimExplorer lets you see and manipulate the raw numbers.

What You See

After generating audio, T5ynth receives the 768-dimensional source-control vectors for your A/B markers. The DimExplorer displays these as a row of vertical bars — one per dimension, sorted by magnitude (the most significant dimensions appear on the left).

  • Each bar shows the difference between Source A and Source B for that dimension. Green bars point toward A, orange bars toward B. The leftmost bars are the dimensions where the two sources differ most — editing here affects the A/B contrast. The rightmost bars are where A and B are nearly identical — editing here changes what both share, often producing more fundamental sonic shifts.
  • If one text field is empty, the absent side is the Echoraum reflection of the present one (2·neutral − other), not a zero vector. The orange/green bars then read as opposing in a structured way: dimensions where the present marker is most articulate are the dimensions where the echo opposes it most strongly.
  • If both fields are empty, A and B are both the neutral encoding, so all bars collapse — there is no A↔B contrast to display.

What You Can Do

Drag any bar vertically to apply a per-dimension offset. Edited bars turn blue. Click "Apply + Generate" to send these offsets to the inference engine, where they are added directly to the internal generation controls used by diffusion.

What This Means

This is the most granular level of control T5ynth offers. It bypasses language entirely — you are directly sculpting the 768-dimensional control vector that guides the diffusion model. What each dimension "does" sonically is largely opaque: the model was not built as a documented audio control surface. Small changes may do nothing, or they may radically alter the output. This is a research tool for probing the space, not a precision instrument.

A compact mini-view of the DimExplorer is always visible below the Axes panel. Click it to open the full interactive overlay. The overlay provides Undo, Redo, and Reset All controls for your edits.

4. Engine Modes: Sampler & Wavetable

T5ynth can play back generated audio in two modes:

Sampler Mode

Plays the generated audio buffer directly. Pitch follows MIDI via time-stretching (Signalsmith Stretch). This mode preserves the full character of the generation.

  • Loop Mode — One-shot, Loop, or Ping-Pong
  • Crossfade — 0–500 ms equal-power crossfade at the loop boundary
  • Loop Optimize — Cross-correlation algorithm that finds the smoothest loop point (Low or High precision)
  • Normalize — Peak-normalizes the play region

Wavetable Mode

Extracts pitch-synchronous frames from the audio to build a scannable wavetable. The Scan slider morphs between frames, and pitch follows MIDI directly (band-limited harmonic synthesis).

  • Scan (0.0–1.0) — Selects the current frame position in the wavetable. Can be modulated by LFOs, envelopes, or drift.

Noise Generator

Alongside the sampler/wavetable source each voice has an independent noise generator that is summed into the voice signal before the filter. It follows the voice's amplitude envelope and note-off just like the main source, so notes including a noise component decay cleanly.

ParameterRangeNotes
Noise Level0.0 – 1.0Mix of the noise generator into the voice signal. 0 disables the noise oscillator entirely.
Noise TypeWhite / Pink / BrownWhite is flat-spectrum (xorshift64 PRNG). Pink rolls off at −3 dB/oct (Paul Kellet approximation). Brown rolls off at −6 dB/oct (leaky integrator). A one-pole DC blocker at ~5 Hz is always applied.

Generated Audio Processing

Before the generated audio reaches the sampler/wavetable source, two filters and one optional gain stage are applied to the raw VAE output:

  • Rumble filter — always on, 2nd-order Butterworth high-pass at 25 Hz. Removes the DC offset and sub-bass rumble the VAE decoder tends to add. Not user-switchable; this is a baseline cleanup step.
  • HF (toggle, in Sampler controls) — two-band high shelf (+3 dB @ 4 kHz, +4.5 dB @ 10 kHz) that compensates the high-frequency rolloff inherent to the VAE decoder. Whether you prefer the brighter boosted or the darker raw sound is a matter of taste; T5ynth stores both.
  • Normalize (toggle, in Sampler controls) — peak-normalizes the play region to 0.95 before playback for quiet generations.

All three operate on the generated buffer, not on the real-time voice signal — re-toggling HF or Normalize re-processes the stored audio from the raw source without regenerating.

Voice Count

Applies to both modes: Mono, 4, 6, 8, 12, or 16 voices. Gain auto-scales per voice count. Oldest notes are stolen when the limit is reached.

Algorithm notes
  • SVF — linear JUCE StateVariableTPTFilter + one-pole cascade. Clean, cheap on CPU, no character of its own. Keep this on low-powered hardware (similar trade-off to choosing the algorithmic reverb over the convolution reverb). Drive applies a pre-filter tanh + optional oversampling, same as previous T5ynth builds. Credit: JUCE DSP.
  • Ladder — Huovilainen-style non-linear 4-pole ladder with a half-sample-delay-compensated feedback path. Analog-flavoured growl, self-oscillating at high resonance, and cutoff-tracking compression. Drive feeds the internal tanh stages directly, so turning drive up produces the "pushed into the ladder" warmth rather than a pre-filter flat-clip. Credit: Antti Huovilainen's DAFx-04 ladder-filter formulation; T5ynth's implementation is original.
  • Warp — zero-delay-feedback 4-pole ladder with a selectable per-pole saturation curve (the Style control). Good for shaping large continuous timbral spaces, which is why it's the recommended target when mapping an internal control dimension to filter character. All six styles are centered at zero, so the feedback loop stays stable under modulation. Warp is the most expensive of the three (~2–3× SVF CPU). Design inspiration: Surge XT Cutoff Warp; T5ynth's implementation is original.
Warp styles — all bounded, all with sat(0) = 0 so the feedback loop has a clean zero equilibrium:
  • Tanh — symmetric soft, the classic reference.
  • SoftClip — x / (1 + |x|); gentler, flatter knee than tanh.
  • OJD — x / √(1 + x²); slower approach to the limit than tanh with more odd-harmonic content for a given drive.
  • Sin — wavefolded sine; FM-like timbre when driven hard.
  • Digital — hard clamp to ±1; deliberately buzzy, rich in odd harmonics at any drive.
  • Asym — tanh(x + 0.25) − tanh(0.25); asymmetric response, prominent 2nd-harmonic colouring, bias-compensated so it's still centered at zero.

6. Drift & Regenerate

Drift & Regenerate keeps the possible-sound space moving.
Without it, you shape one space, generate one fragment, and then process that fragment with conventional synth tools. With Drift & Regenerate, the hidden sound space itself evolves. The modulation system shifts generation parameters over time, triggering new inference runs in the background. The source is no longer a static waveform — it follows a changing path through the model's internal sound space.

The Asynchronous Feedback Loop

Generation is not tied to the audio callback. Depending on model, duration, step count, and hardware, a regeneration can arrive in roughly 0.1 seconds on an RTX 6000-class GPU or take several seconds on a Mac M-series processor without a dedicated AI-capable graphics card. T5ynth creates an asynchronous feedback loop between the modulation system and the possible-sound space that keeps the instrument playing while new fragments are rendered in the background:

While the synth is playing, multiple drift LFOs continuously and independently shift Alpha and the Semantic Axes. Each LFO moves at its own rate and depth — Alpha might slowly sweep from A toward B over 30 seconds, while Axis 1 (for example tonal/noisy) oscillates on a different cycle, and Axis 2 (for example sustained/rhythmic) drifts at yet another pace. With a fixed seed, this remains reproducible. With Random active, each regeneration receives a new seed: the exact sound will not repeat, but the semantic poles keep the material inside the marked space.

When an auto regen mode is active, T5ynth monitors how far the modulated generation controls have drifted from the last generated state. Once the drift exceeds a threshold, it fires a new inference request in the background. The current sound keeps playing uninterrupted. When the new generation arrives, it is crossfaded into the playback buffer, replacing the old sound with the new one without stopping playback.

The result is a continuous stream of new generations, each one produced from a slightly different internal state between and around the two impulses. With Random active, no generation is simply repeated; without Random, the same seed and same state remain recallable. In both cases, the movement is not arbitrary: it follows the trajectories defined by the drift LFO settings inside the sound space opened by the two impulses.

Meanwhile, the conventional synth chain (filter, envelopes, LFOs, effects, sequencer) continues to process the output in real time. You are simultaneously shaping the sound and the process that creates the sound.

The Drift section provides three slow LFOs (0.001–2.0 Hz) designed for organic, evolving textures. Unlike the main LFOs, drift can target generation-level parameters — Alpha, the three sound-character axes, Noise, and Magnitude. This is what makes drift unique: it doesn't just modulate how the sound is played back, it modulates what sound is generated.

GEN runs up to five parallel Strands (Strand 1 is always active; Strands 2–5 enable individually). Four secondary strands plus the reference voice means each of the four roles (Anchor / Line / Density / Gesture) can be assigned to its own strand alongside Strand 1, without having to drop one. 12. Strategies for Finding Sweet Spots

T5ynth is not a sweet-spot synth. Unlike subtractive synths where most parameter combinations produce usable sound, T5ynth's generation process is exploratory and unpredictable. Many combinations produce nothing interesting. That's by design — the instrument rewards patient exploration, not preset-surfing.

1. Start with the source

Get a generation you like before touching synth parameters. A great filter envelope cannot save a bad generation. Experiment with A/B texts, seeds, and short durations (1–3s) to quickly iterate.

2. Use low step counts for speed

While exploring, keep Steps low to iterate quickly. With Stable Audio Open Small, 8 steps is already the ceiling — more steps add nothing. With Stable Audio Open 1.0, start at 20 and only go higher once you've found a direction worth refining.

3. Exploit the seed

Same A/B text + different seed = very different audio. If a marker feels right but the result doesn't, change the seed before changing the wording. Generate 5–10 seeds before concluding a marker doesn't work.

4. Alpha for morphing, not mixing

Alpha is not a volume crossfade. It moves through the model's hidden sound space — the midpoint (0.0) is often not a blend of A and B but something new. Use Alpha to discover unexpected sounds between your two impulses. Push it past ±1.0 to extrapolate. If you leave one text field empty, Alpha sweeps from your impulse through the model-neutral midpoint to its antipode — the Echoraum trajectory, sounds the impulse actively isn't.

5. One axis at a time

Sound-character axes are most effective used individually. Stacking three axes at extreme values dilutes each one. Start with one axis and learn its response before adding a second.

6. Sampler and Wavetable as different pitch behaviours

Sampler mode follows MIDI with pitch-preserving transposition and keeps the generated fragment's full temporal character. Wavetable mode extracts pitch-synchronous frames for a more oscillator-like pitch response. Choose by sound and motion, not because only one of them can play pitch.

7. Short loops + Scan modulation

Generate 1–2s of audio, switch to Wavetable, and modulate Scan with a slow LFO or Env1. This creates evolving timbres from a single generation — the classic wavetable synthesis approach, but with AI-generated waves.

8. Drift for evolution, not randomness

Set Drift LFO rates very low (0.01–0.05 Hz) and depths moderate (0.2–0.5). Target Alpha or an Axis. Enable Auto-regen. This activates the asynchronous feedback loop — the synth slowly transforms itself, producing a continuous stream of new generations. Far more interesting than random Noise injection.

9. Gen Seq + Arp as a discovery engine

Turn on the generative sequencer with moderate mutation (0.3–0.5), pick a scale, and let it run. Add the arpeggiator in Up mode at 1/16. The combination reveals how your sound behaves across pitch ranges and rhythmic contexts — a rapid audition tool.

10. Save early, save often

Presets include the generated audio, so you never lose a good generation. Export a preset the moment you find something promising. You can always refine the synth parameters later — but the generation itself is hard to reproduce without the exact seed and settings.

13. How Semantic Axes Work

T5ynth's semantic axes are directions in the 768-dimensional embedding space of the T5 text encoder. Here's how they are constructed:

Step 1: Define Poles

Each axis is defined by two single-word anchors representing opposing concepts — for example, "noise" and "music". These are not arbitrary labels; they were selected through systematic testing of which word-pairs produce the most audibly distinct results.

Step 2: Encode Poles + Neutral

Three embeddings are computed via the T5 encoder:

  • emb_pole_a — encoding of the negative pole (e.g., "noise")
  • emb_pole_b — encoding of the positive pole (e.g., "music")
  • emb_neutral — encoding of an empty text field ("")

Step 3: Compute Direction

The axis direction is the difference between a pole and the neutral point:

  • Positive values: direction = emb_pole_b − emb_neutral
  • Negative values: direction = emb_pole_a − emb_neutral

This direction vector is then scaled by the slider value and added to the current manipulated embedding.

Step 4: Validation

Not all conceptual oppositions produce audible differences. The 8 axes included in T5ynth were validated by generating audio at each pole and measuring Mel-spectrogram cosine distance. Only axes with a distance above 0.70 at 1-second duration survived. PCA-based axes (derived from variance in the embedding space rather than semantic opposition) were tested but found to collapse at short durations — they produce near-identical audio regardless of slider position.

In the code, axis direction vectors are summed onto the embedding. But because the diffusion model processes the resulting embedding non-linearly, the audible effect is not simply additive — multiple axes are interrelated and can interfere with each other unpredictably. There is no orthogonalization, and axes may partially overlap in embedding space.

14. The DimExplorer In Depth

The DimExplorer (see section 3 for basic usage) operates on the mean-pooled T5 encoder output — the 768-dimensional vector obtained by averaging across the token sequence dimension, weighted by the attention mask. This is the same representation the diffusion model uses for conditioning.

Why Sorted by Magnitude?

Sorting the 768 bars by |emb_A[i] − emb_B[i]| is not just a display convenience — it creates two distinct editing zones:

  • Left side (high magnitude) — dimensions where A and B differ most. Editing here affects what distinguishes your two impulses from each other.
  • Right side (low magnitude) — dimensions where A and B are nearly identical, i.e., what they have in common. Editing here tends to produce more fundamental changes to the overall character of the sound, since you are altering the shared foundation rather than the A/B contrast.

What Does a Dimension Represent?

Individual T5 dimensions do not correspond to human-interpretable audio features. The T5 model was trained on language tasks (not audio), and its internal structure encodes statistical patterns in text — not timbral qualities, pitch, or rhythm. The Stable Audio model learned to map these text patterns to sound, but the mapping is opaque and non-linear. A single dimension might contribute to brightness in one A/B context and to rhythmic density in another. This is why the DimExplorer is a research tool: it rewards experimentation, not prediction.

Technical Details

  • Offsets are applied as manipulated[:, :, idx] += offset_value — broadcast across all tokens in the sequence
  • Only non-zero offsets are transmitted to the inference engine (sparse representation)
  • Offsets persist across generations until explicitly reset
  • The edit history (undo/redo) stores complete offset state snapshots

15. Principles of the Generative Sequencer

The generative sequencer combines three layers of musical algorithm:

Layer 1: Euclidean Rhythm

The rhythmic backbone uses Bjorklund's algorithm (Toussaint, 2005) to distribute N pulses across M steps as evenly as possible. This is the same mathematical principle behind many traditional rhythmic patterns worldwide — it produces patterns that feel natural because they maximize spacing regularity.

Example: 5 pulses in 8 steps → [x . x . x . x x] — a pattern equivalent to Cuban tresillo.

The Rotation parameter shifts this pattern circularly, changing which beat feels like "one."

Layer 2: Stride-Based Melody

Pulses are assigned pitches using a stride permutation through the selected scale. The stride value (max(1, steps − pulses)) determines how many scale degrees to skip between successive notes, chosen to be coprime with the total number of degrees so the melody visits as many pitches as possible before repeating.

The traversal follows a triangle wave (ascending then descending), creating bidirectional melodic motion rather than monotonic climbing. This produces melodies that feel more natural and song-like.

Velocity is derived from the rhythmic gaps — notes after longer rests receive higher velocity, creating natural accents at the start of rhythmic phrases.

Layer 3: Turing Machine Mutation

Each time the pattern completes a cycle, Mutation determines how many notes change. At mutation rate 0.8 with 16 pulses, roughly 8 notes shift by 1–4 scale degrees. The result: the pattern stays recognizable but continuously evolves — like a melodic idea being rethought in real time.

When the Mutation Fix toggle is unlocked, the mutation amount itself breathes over a fixed internal 16-cycle sine period. The Evolve slider sets the base amount; the breathing then moves around that value. This is separate from the Pitch Field Rate control, which is user-settable from 1 to 32 cycles.

Parameter Drift

When the Fix toggles are unlocked, the Euclidean parameters themselves drift over 8-cycle windows:

  • Rotation shifts circularly (timing determined by a secondary Euclidean distribution)
  • Pulses increase or decrease over time, changing pattern density
  • Steps expand or contract while preserving the pulse-to-step ratio

All drift is bounded and reversible — parameters oscillate rather than running away. The combination of melodic mutation, mutation breathing, and parameter drift produces patterns that can evolve for minutes without becoming monotonous or losing coherence.

Layer 4: Polyphonic Strands

Up to five Strands run in parallel, each with its own Euclidean DNA, drift state, role, and tempo multiplier. Strands are not voices in a chord — they are independent agents sharing one pitch space. The polyrhythmic interplay arises naturally from the sorted Div× multipliers (1/16×, 1/8×, 1/5×, 1/4×, 1/3×, 1/2×, 1×, 2×, 3×, 4×, 5×, 8×, 16×), which create non-coincident downbeats; cycle boundaries only realign on the lowest common multiple. Each strand runs the same internal pipeline as Strand 1 (Euclidean rhythm + Turing degree-walk + quantization to its register); the role choice only sets default character. How strands interact (or don't) is decided by the Coordination Mode; see §9. With four secondary strands available, every role (Anchor, Line, Density, Gesture) can be assigned uniquely if desired.

Each strand's Role selects a different note-picking strategy from the shared field:

  • Anchor — low-register support. Walks the field via its degree pattern; Dominance probabilistically snaps the note to centerPc at the top of every other cycle (cadential anchor, not a per-beat tic).
  • Line — mid-register voice. Walks the field from its degree pattern.
  • Density — mid-register, shorter gate. On weak steps may take a single chromatic step toward the next field-member landing; the strong-step note remains a field member.
  • Gesture — wider register. Sparse pulses on its own Euclidean pattern.

The Dominance parameter (per strand) controls how strongly the strand returns to centerPc at cycle-pair boundaries. At dominance 0 the strand follows its degree walk freely; at 1.0 the cycle-start note becomes centerPc. There is no per-event harmonic check between strands — that responsibility is moved to the Coordination Mode.

Layer 5: The Pitch Field

All strands draw from a single shared Pitch Field — a 12-bit pitch-class set (pcSet) plus an ordered row used by Transform mode. The field replaces classical chord/scale context with something more flexible: an evolving collection of allowed pitch classes that can transform through several distinct algorithmic paradigms.

The field evolves on Strand 1's cycle boundary at the chosen Rate (1–32 cycles between changes). Four modes:

  • Static — derived from Scale Root + Scale Type, never changes. Use when you want the harmonic field stable while only rhythm and mutation evolve.
  • Drift — each tick, one pitch class is removed and one chromatic pitch class is added (the center pc is protected from removal). Slow harmonic motion that stays close to the starting set.
  • Transform — applies a random twelve-tone row operation each tick: transposition (Tn), inversion (In), retrograde (R), or retrograde-inversion (RI). The new pcSet is then derived from the transformed row's first rowSize members.
  • Pivot — transposes the entire pc-set by the configured Pivot interval (m3, M3, Tritone, P5) each tick, accumulating shift over time. After enough cycles the field returns to its origin (e.g. four cycles at m3 = full octave).

Because all strands share the same field, harmonic coherence is provided at the field level alone: a Drift swap or Transform tick changes what every strand can play. There is no per-event harmonic check between strands — that question is delegated to the Coordination Mode (§9), which currently caps simultaneous voices via priority displacement (DensityBudget). The listener perceives one evolving texture because the strands draw from the same evolving pitch set, not because each note is negotiated against the others.

Roles, dominance, and field modes operate without a Bass/Lead/Pad hierarchy or chord-root tracking. The intent is to produce evolving textures independent of functional harmony. For diatonic results, set Field Mode = Static, Dominance high, and one strand to Anchor.

The important point in this overview is the position in the signal flow: Injection Modes change what the diffusion model receives as its generative control state. They are not sampler, wavetable, filter, or effect modes.

Key observations from this flow:

  • Impulse A/B, Alpha, Injection Modes, Semantic Axes, Magnitude, Noise, and DimExplorer offsets shape the control path that guides generation.
  • Filter, envelopes, LFOs, effects operate after generation — they are traditional synth processing on the generated audio.
  • Drift LFOs bridge both worlds — they can modulate pre-generation parameters (Alpha, Semantic Axes, Noise, Magnitude) and post-generation parameters (Filter, Scan).
  • The Python inference subprocess stays alive between generations — no startup overhead.
  • Determinism is guaranteed across platforms (same seed + settings = identical audio on Mac, Linux, GPU, or CPU) via a patched numpy PCG64 random number generator.

17. How Injection Modes Work

The user-facing injection-mode buttons (§1) are a thin UI on top of two specific manipulation points inside the Stable Audio Open diffusion pipeline. This section explains those points carefully so the modes are predictable rather than mysterious.

What "injection" means here

The Stable Audio Open diffusion transformer (DiT) consumes the text-conditioning embedding via cross-attention: every one of its 16 transformer blocks contains a cross_attn layer that mixes the audio latent with a projected version of the T5-encoded text. The cross-attention conditioning tensor enters the DiT once per sampler step, gets projected through a to_cond_embed layer, and is then fed identically to every block.

"Injection" in T5ynth means: instead of conditioning the DiT with a single static A↔B blend, we manipulate the conditioning tensor along two orthogonal axes:

  1. Time (sampler steps) — replace the conditioning at a specific sampler step. Early steps see one tensor; late steps see another. This is the "Step-in" axis.
  2. Depth (DiT block index) — replace the conditioning per block. Different blocks see different tensors at the same time. This is the "Layer" axis.

The Combo modes combine both axes simultaneously: at a given step, each of the 16 blocks may see a different mix.

The two PyTorch hooks that do the work

Both axes are implemented as Python-level monkey-patches and hooks applied to the loaded diffusion pipeline before sampling, then removed at the end of each generation. The C++ side never sees them — it only sets the mode string and parameters in the request.

  • Sampler-step counter — the pipeline's DiTWrapper.forward is replaced with a closure that increments a counter state["calls"] after every call. Because the wrapper is invoked exactly once per sampler step (with both CFG halves batched), the counter equals the current step index. Late-step modes compare it against transition_step = max(1, round(steps · injection_transition_at)) to decide whether the early or late conditioning is active.
  • Per-block forward_pre_hooks on each block.cross_attn. The hook intercepts the runtime context kwarg (the to_cond_embed-projected conditioning, doubled for CFG) and returns a replacement tensor. The replacement is pre-projected through the same to_cond_embed layer once at request start, so the hook bypasses projection and just swaps the post-projection context.

ModeTime axisDepth axisMask shape

Why hard mask in Combo but soft mask in Layer

Layer mode lets the user drag a two-thumb range slider, and a sigmoid top-hat (soft 4-layer ramps at each edge) means small slider movements produce continuous changes — a gentler control surface for live exploration. Combo modes, by contrast, are presets: the band is fixed by the mode itself, the user only controls the intensity slider. There is nothing to interpolate. A hard mask (w=1 inside the band, 0 outside) makes "slider=1" mean exactly what users expect — 100 % B in those specific blocks. Empirically, a 4-block-wide Layer band peaks at w≈0.534 in the centre with the soft mask; the hard mask version of the same 4 blocks reads as a far stronger preset effect even at moderate slider settings.

Why these particular layer bands

The cross-attention layers in the SA Open Small DiT are not interchangeable — they appear to perform different work at different depths. The current Combo tuples (Combo 1 = blocks 0..3, Combo 2 = blocks 4..11, Combo 3 = blocks 6..9) reflect listening-test findings:

  • Surface (low blocks) — narrow effect. Influences the surface "skin" / texture of the output without changing its overall shape much. This is the Combo 1 band.
  • Mid (broad) — high conditioning leverage. The middle 8 blocks carry most of the diffusion's text-following work, so injecting B there reshapes the result substantially. This is the Combo 2 band.
  • High (top 4) — surprisingly low leverage. The original plan placed Combo 2 at blocks 12..15 to mirror Combo 1's "skin" framing, but listening tests revealed the top blocks are dominated by refinement work and barely register cross-attention overrides. Combo 2 was moved to broad-mid.
  • Center (narrow) — same width as Combo 1 at a different vertical position. This is Combo 3, the "where-versus-width" probe.

These bands are subject to revision as more listening data accrues. The backend/pipe_inference.py handler is the authoritative source; the C++ side sends matching values purely for log/preset legibility.

What stays unchanged

  • Magnitude, Noise, Dimension Offsets, Semantic Axes all reach the DiT in every mode. They are applied to whichever embedding tensor(s) the active mode actually feeds into the cross-attention layer — the global blend in Linear / Delta, both the early A path and the late blend in Step-in, both A and B (independently, with the same shifts) before projection in Layer, and A and the late blend before projection in Combo 1/2/3. The same noise sample and the same axis / dim shifts are reused across paths so the per-mode geometry behaves predictably (e.g. a Linear-style A↔B blend would still receive noise once, not per term).
  • Drift targets the active mode's intensity axis automatically. No new drift targets are needed.
  • Determinism — the hooks are deterministic; they do not introduce additional RNG. Same seed + same A/B text + same mode and slider position = identical audio across platforms.
  • Linear remains the canonical default — bit-for-bit identical to T5ynth v1.5.x. The non-linear modes are research-mode controls, layered on top.
