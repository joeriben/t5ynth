T5ynth

A text-to-audio synthesizer that navigates the T5 embedding space of a diffusion audio model.
This guide covers how to use T5ynth and the ideas behind it.
Transparency note: this manual was drafted by Claude (Anthropic) and edited by the human author.

T5ynth is a JUCE-based synthesizer plugin (Standalone / VST3 / AU) that deliberately repurposes Stability AI's Stable Audio Open model as an oscillator — not to generate finished music, but to produce raw sonic material for human–nonhuman artistic collaboration. Where text-to-audio models are designed to substitute creative labor (type a description, get a result), T5ynth inverts this relationship: the AI produces material, the human creates. This is not a consumer tool — it is an instrument that requires musicianship.

T5ynth is a personal side-project by Prof. Dr. Benjamin Jörissen, UNESCO Chair in Digital Culture and Arts in Education (UCDCAE), Friedrich-Alexander-Universität Erlangen-Nürnberg, and part of the UCDCAE AI Lab Software Collection.

  • AI for Arts Education (AI4ArtsEd), conducted together with the University of Cologne and the German Research Institute for Artificial Intelligence (DFKI) Kaiserslautern
  • ComeArts Across, a research project for the development of digital cultural teacher education

Both funded by the Federal Ministry for Education, Family, Senior Citizens, Women and Youth (BMBFSFJ).

Setup: Model Installation

Model Download

T5ynth requires at least one diffusion model. Models are not bundled — they must be downloaded separately.

Open the Settings panel from the status bar, pick a model from the dropdown, and read the per-model instructions shown there. The summary below tells you which model to pick.

Stable Audio Open Small

Licensed under the Stability AI Community License. Gated on HuggingFace — a free HuggingFace account is required once to accept the license and download the files. No terminal required.

  1. In Settings, click Open Model Page. Your browser opens the HuggingFace page for the model.
  2. Sign up or log in on HuggingFace (free account).
  3. Click Agree and access repository to accept the license.
  4. Open the Files and versions tab and download exactly these two files to your usual Downloads folder:
    • model.safetensors (~1.56 GB)
    • model_config.json
    Do not download model.ckpt, base_model.ckpt, base_model.safetensors, or base_model_config.json — those are alternative formats T5ynth does not use.
  5. Back in T5ynth, click Auto-Scan. T5ynth finds the two files in your Downloads folder and copies them to the right place. You can delete the originals from Downloads afterwards.

If you saved the files to a folder other than Downloads, Auto-Scan opens a folder picker and asks you to point at the folder.

AudioLDM2

AudioLDM2 is an academic latent-diffusion text-to-audio model published by the Centre for Vision, Speech and Signal Processing (CVSSP) at the University of Surrey together with collaborators; see the paper "AudioLDM 2: Learning Holistic Audio Generation with Self-supervised Pretraining" (Liu et al., 2023). It was released as an open research artefact to study generalised audio, music, and speech generation from text, not as a commercial product. It is ungated on HuggingFace and the only engine T5ynth can install for you directly — in Settings, select AudioLDM2 and click Download from HuggingFace.

Model: cvssp/audioldm2. License: CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 — non-commercial use only, no revenue threshold, no exceptions. T5ynth includes AudioLDM2 as an alternative sound source for non-commercial musical exploration, particularly for users who do not want to create a HuggingFace account.

Stable Audio Open 1.0

Licensed under the Stability AI Community License. Gated on HuggingFace — a free HuggingFace account is required once to accept the license. Device Selection

T5ynth auto-detects the best available compute device:

  • Apple Silicon Mac → MPS (Metal Performance Shaders), ~3× faster than CPU
  • NVIDIA GPU on Linux or Windows 11 → CUDA
  • No GPU → CPU fallback (works, but slow)

You can override the device in the generation section. The device toggle switches between GPU and CPU.

First Generation

On first launch, the status bar shows backend connection status. Once you see "Ready", type a prompt and click Generate. The first generation takes longer because the model is loaded into GPU memory on demand. Subsequent generations are faster.

If the status bar shows an error, check: (1) Is at least one model downloaded? (2) Is there enough disk space and RAM/VRAM? Stable Audio Open 1.0 needs ~4 GB VRAM.
Part I

1. The Generation Section (Oscillator)

T5ynth does not have a traditional oscillator. Instead, it repurposes a text-to-audio AI model as a sound source — not to generate finished music on command, but to produce raw sonic material that you shape as a musician. The diffusion model (Stable Audio) becomes an oscillator whose output is steered by navigating a 768-dimensional embedding space. This is a deliberate inversion: the AI is not the creator, you are. The model provides material; you make music from it.

Prompts A & B

You have two text input fields. Each prompt is encoded into a 768-dimensional embedding vector by a T5 language model. To understand what that means: an embedding is a way of representing the meaning of a text as a point in a high-dimensional space. Words and phrases that are semantically related end up close together; unrelated concepts end up far apart. Every dimension captures some aspect of meaning — not a single human-readable feature, but a learned statistical pattern. (For an excellent visual introduction, see Jay Alammar's Illustrated Word2Vec.)

These embedding vectors — not the text itself — are what steers the diffusion model. T5ynth operates on them as geometric objects: moving, blending, scaling, and perturbing them before any audio is generated.

  • Two prompts — enter text in both A and B. The Alpha slider (labeled A ↔ B) interpolates between them in embedding space.
  • One prompt + Echoraum — leave one field empty. The empty field becomes the reflection of the present prompt through the model's neutral encoding (the encoding of "", also used as emb_neutral in the Semantic Axes math): geometrically 2·neutral − other. Alpha then traces a path from the prompt through the model-neutral midpoint to the prompt's tonal antipode — sounds the prompt actively isn't. A and B are peers; either side may be the empty one.
  • Both empty — both embeddings collapse to the neutral encoding. The model produces its own self-portrait (pure unconditional sampling), varying with the seed.
Prompts should describe sound qualities, not musical genres. "A warm resonant drone with metallic harmonics" works better than "ambient techno." The model has no reliable concept of genre.
Leaving a field empty is an active musical decision, not a fallback. If you want the same character regardless of the A ↔ B slider, type the same text in both fields — that is mathematically equivalent to a pinned single-prompt sound (since 0.5·A + 0.5·A = A for every value of Alpha).

Generation Controls

ControlRangeWhat It Does
Alpha (A ↔ B) -2.0 … +2.0 Navigates between Prompt A (−1) and Prompt B (+1) in embedding space, not in audio. This is not a crossfade or mix of two audio signals — it is movement through a semantic space. The midpoint (0) is not "half A plus half B" in any audible sense; it is a new point in meaning-space that the model interprets independently. For example, if Prompt A is "bowed cello, sustained" and Prompt B is "crackling fire", the midpoint will not sound like cello mixed with fire — it might produce something like a warm, textured, resonant hum that resembles neither source directly. Values beyond ±1 extrapolate — pushing past either pole into regions of the embedding space that no text prompt would naturally reach. When one prompt field is left empty, the empty side is the Echoraum reflection of the other, so Alpha sweeps from the prompt through the model-neutral midpoint to its antipode (see the Prompts section). The slider uses a quadratic curve for fine control near the center.
Magnitude 0.001 … 5.0 Scales the embedding vector's length. At 1.0 the prompt influence is normal. Lower values drift toward the model's "average" sound; higher values push the model harder (can get unstable above ~2.0).
Noise 0.0 … 1.0 This is not audio noise — it is semantic noise: random perturbation in meaning-space, a dose of chaos applied to the embedding before the model ever generates a sound. Even small values (0.05–0.15) introduce subtle variation between otherwise identical generations. Higher values scramble the prompt — useful for exploration, less for precision.
Duration 0.1 … 11 s Length of generated audio. T5ynth targets short sound material, not finished music, so the slider is capped at 11 s — the maximum output length of Stable Audio Open Small. Short values (0.5–3 s) work well for loops and wavetable sources. Longer values create evolving textures.
Steps 1 … 100 Diffusion denoising iterations. More steps = higher quality but slower. The useful range depends on the model: Stable Audio Open 1.0 benefits from 20–100 steps (20 is a good default, higher values refine detail). Stable Audio Open Small converges quickly — above 8 steps there is no positive effect.
CFG Scale 1.0 … 15.0 Classifier-Free Guidance — how strictly the model follows your prompt. Model-dependent: Stable Audio Open 1.0 works well at 5–8 (default 7). Stable Audio Open Small should stay at 1 — higher values produce artifacts rather than better adherence. In general: low values are loose and dreamy, high values are literal but can sound harsh.
Start Position 0.0 … 1.0 Inpainting offset within the diffusion timeline. 0.0 gives you the attack/onset of a sound. ~0.5–0.7 gives you the sustained body, skipping transients — if there were any in the generated sample.
Seed -1 … 999 999 999 Controls random state. Same seed + same settings = identical output (cross-platform). Use the Random button to automatically pick a new seed on each generation.
HF Boost on / off Compensates for high-frequency rolloff inherent to the VAE decoder. Whether you prefer the brighter boosted or the darker raw sound is a matter of taste.
Auto on / off Automatic re-generation after every parameter change in the oscillator section.
This is not a static oscillator. The generation parameters above — Alpha, Axes, Noise, Magnitude — can all be modulated by the Drift & Regenerate system. With Drift active, these values shift continuously, and T5ynth can automatically re-generate audio as they move. The T5 oscillator becomes a continuously evolving sound source, not a one-shot sample generator. See section 6 for details.

Model & Device

Three model slots are available: Stable Audio Open 1.0, Stable Audio Open Small, and AudioLDM2 (experimental, CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 — non-commercial only). The device toggle switches between GPU (MPS on Apple Silicon, CUDA on Linux or Windows 11) and CPU. GPU is typically 3× faster on Apple Silicon.

2. Semantic Axes

Below the prompt area, three dropdown slots let you apply semantic axes — continuous sliders between two conceptual poles. Each axis nudges the embedding in a specific direction within the 768-dimensional T5 space.

AxisLeft Pole (−1)Right Pole (+1)
noise / musicnoisemusic
electronic / acousticelectronicacoustic
composed / improvisedcomposedimprovised
raw / refinedrawrefined
ensemble / soloensemblesolo
secular / sacredsecularsacred
noisy / tonalnoisytonal
sustained / rhythmicsustainedrhythmic

Each slider runs from −1.0 to +1.0. Multiple axes are interrelated — the more you stack, the less predictable each individual axis becomes.

These 8 axes were experimentally validated via spectral analysis (Mel-cosine distance, 1s vs 10s duration, on RTX 6000). The top semantic axes scored Mel distance > 0.80 at 1s — meaning they produce clearly audible differences even in very short samples. PCA-based axes were tested but collapse at short durations (Mel < 0.19) and are not offered. The labels are approximate — they describe tendencies, not guarantees.

3. Dimension Explorer

The Dimension Explorer gives you direct access to all 768 individual dimensions of the T5 embedding space. While prompts and semantic axes offer human-readable ways to navigate this space, the DimExplorer lets you see and manipulate the raw numbers.

What You See

After generating audio, T5ynth receives the 768-dimensional embedding vectors for your prompts. The DimExplorer displays these as a row of vertical bars — one per dimension, sorted by magnitude (the most significant dimensions appear on the left).

  • Each bar shows the difference between Embedding A and Embedding B for that dimension. Green bars point toward A, orange bars toward B. The leftmost bars are the dimensions where the two embeddings differ most — editing here affects the A/B contrast. The rightmost bars are where A and B are nearly identical — editing here changes what both share, often producing more fundamental sonic shifts.
  • If one prompt field is empty, the absent side is the Echoraum reflection of the present one (2·neutral − other), not a zero vector. The orange/green bars then read as opposing in a structured way: dimensions where the prompt is most articulate are the dimensions where the echo opposes it most strongly.
  • If both fields are empty, A and B are both the neutral encoding, so all bars collapse — there is no A↔B contrast to display.

What You Can Do

Drag any bar vertically to apply a per-dimension offset. Edited bars turn blue. Click "Apply + Generate" to send these offsets to the inference engine, where they are added directly to the embedding before diffusion runs.

What This Means

This is the most granular level of control T5ynth offers. It bypasses language entirely — you are directly sculpting the 768-dimensional vector that guides the diffusion model. What each dimension "does" sonically is largely opaque: the T5 embedding space was trained for language tasks, not audio, and its internal structure does not map to perceptual audio categories in any documented way. Small changes may do nothing, or they may radically alter the output. This is a research tool for probing the space, not a precision instrument.

A compact mini-view of the DimExplorer is always visible below the Axes panel. Click it to open the full interactive overlay. The overlay provides Undo, Redo, and Reset All controls for your edits.

4. Engine Modes: Sampler & Wavetable

T5ynth can play back generated audio in two modes:

Sampler Mode

Plays the generated audio buffer directly. Pitch follows MIDI via time-stretching (Signalsmith Stretch). This mode preserves the full character of the generation.

  • Loop Mode — One-shot, Loop, or Ping-Pong
  • Crossfade — 0–500 ms equal-power crossfade at the loop boundary
  • Loop Optimize — Cross-correlation algorithm that finds the smoothest loop point (Low or High precision)
  • Normalize — Peak-normalizes the play region

Wavetable Mode

Extracts pitch-synchronous frames from the audio to build a scannable wavetable. The Scan slider morphs between frames, and pitch follows MIDI directly (band-limited harmonic synthesis).

  • Scan (0.0–1.0) — Selects the current frame position in the wavetable. Can be modulated by LFOs, envelopes, or drift.

Noise Generator

Alongside the sampler/wavetable source each voice has an independent noise generator that is summed into the voice signal before the filter. It follows the voice's amplitude envelope and note-off just like the main source, so notes including a noise component decay cleanly.

ParameterRangeNotes
Noise Level0.0 – 1.0Mix of the noise generator into the voice signal. 0 disables the noise oscillator entirely.
Noise TypeWhite / Pink / BrownWhite is flat-spectrum (xorshift64 PRNG). Pink rolls off at −3 dB/oct (Paul Kellet approximation). Brown rolls off at −6 dB/oct (leaky integrator). A one-pole DC blocker at ~5 Hz is always applied.

Generated Audio Processing

Before the generated audio reaches the sampler/wavetable source, two filters and one optional gain stage are applied to the raw VAE output:

  • Rumble filter — always on, 2nd-order Butterworth high-pass at 25 Hz. Removes the DC offset and sub-bass rumble the VAE decoder tends to add. Not user-switchable; this is a baseline cleanup step.
  • HF (toggle, in Sampler controls) — two-band high shelf (+3 dB @ 4 kHz, +4.5 dB @ 10 kHz) that compensates the high-frequency rolloff inherent to the VAE decoder. Whether you prefer the brighter boosted or the darker raw sound is a matter of taste; T5ynth stores both.
  • Normalize (toggle, in Sampler controls) — peak-normalizes the play region to 0.95 before playback, useful for quiet generations.

All three operate on the generated buffer, not on the real-time voice signal — re-toggling HF or Normalize re-processes the stored audio from the raw source without regenerating.

Voice Count

Applies to both modes: Mono, 4, 6, 8, 12, or 16 voices. Gain auto-scales per voice count. Oldest notes are stolen when the limit is reached.

Algorithm notes
  • SVF — linear JUCE StateVariableTPTFilter + one-pole cascade. Clean, cheap on CPU, no character of its own. Keep this on low-powered hardware (similar trade-off to choosing the algorithmic reverb over the convolution reverb). Drive applies a pre-filter tanh + optional oversampling, same as previous T5ynth builds.
  • Ladder — Huovilainen-style non-linear 4-pole Moog ladder with a half-sample-delay-compensated feedback path. Classic analog-flavoured growl, self-oscillating at high resonance, and the characteristic Moog cutoff-tracking compression. Drive feeds the internal tanh stages directly, so turning drive up produces the "pushed into the ladder" warmth rather than a pre-filter flat-clip.
  • Warp — Surge-XT-style zero-delay-feedback 4-pole ladder with a selectable per-pole saturation curve (the Style control). Good for shaping large continuous timbral spaces, which is why it's the recommended target when mapping an embedding dimension to filter character. All six styles are centered at zero, so the feedback loop stays stable under modulation. Warp is the most expensive of the three (~2–3× SVF CPU).
Warp styles — all bounded, all with sat(0) = 0 so the feedback loop has a clean zero equilibrium:
  • Tanh — symmetric soft, the classic reference.
  • SoftClip — x / (1 + |x|); gentler, flatter knee than tanh.
  • OJD — x / √(1 + x²); slower approach to the limit than tanh with more odd-harmonic content for a given drive.
  • Sin — wavefolded sine; FM-like timbre when driven hard.
  • Digital — hard clamp to ±1; deliberately buzzy, rich in odd harmonics at any drive.
  • Asym — tanh(x + 0.25) − tanh(0.25); asymmetric response, prominent 2nd-harmonic colouring, bias-compensated so it's still centered at zero.

6. Drift & Regenerate

Drift & Regenerate is what makes T5ynth more than a sample player or wavetable synth.
Without it, you generate a sound and then shape it with conventional synth tools — the source stays fixed. With Drift & Regenerate, the sound source itself continuously evolves. The modulation system shifts generation parameters over time, triggering new inference runs in the background. The oscillator is no longer a static waveform — it traces a continuous trajectory through the 768-dimensional embedding space.

The Asynchronous Feedback Loop

Diffusion inference is never real-time — a single generation takes seconds, not milliseconds. But T5ynth creates an asynchronous feedback loop between the modulation system and the T5 oscillator that keeps you in the flow:

While the synth is playing, multiple drift LFOs continuously and independently shift the Alpha slider and the semantic axes. Each LFO moves at its own rate and depth — Alpha might slowly sweep from Prompt A toward B over 30 seconds, while Axis 1 (e.g., tonal/noisy) oscillates on a different cycle, and Axis 2 (e.g., sustained/rhythmic) drifts at yet another pace. These modulations run simultaneously and asynchronously, tracing a complex, never-repeating path through the embedding space.

When an auto regen mode is active, T5ynth monitors how far the modulated embedding has drifted from the last generated state. Once the drift exceeds a threshold, it fires a new inference request in the background. The current sound keeps playing uninterrupted. When the new generation arrives — typically a few seconds later — it is crossfaded into the playback buffer, smoothly replacing the old sound with the new one.

The result is a continuous stream of new generations, each one produced from a slightly different position in the embedding space between and around the two prompts. The sound never loops back to exactly where it was. Every few seconds, a new variation emerges — not randomly, but along the trajectories defined by your drift LFO settings. You hear a sound that perpetually transforms: sometimes closer to Prompt A, sometimes to B, sometimes in between, colored by whichever semantic axes are drifting, at whatever pace you have set.

Meanwhile, the conventional synth chain (filter, envelopes, LFOs, effects, sequencer) continues to process the output in real time. You are simultaneously shaping the sound and the process that creates the sound.

The Drift section provides three slow LFOs (0.001–2.0 Hz) designed for organic, evolving textures. Unlike the main LFOs, drift can target generation-level parameters — Alpha, the three semantic axes, Noise, and Magnitude. This is what makes drift unique: it doesn't just modulate how the sound is played back, it modulates what sound is generated.

The beat-limited modes (max 4♩, max 16♩) help keep compute and energy use in check. Consider whether Manual mode is sufficient for your purpose.

8. Step Sequencer

A traditional step sequencer with 2–32 steps. Each step has:

  • Note — MIDI note 0–127 (draggable vertical slider)

Press the GEN button to switch from the manual step sequencer to the generative mode. This is a self-evolving pattern engine that combines Euclidean rhythms with Turing Machine–style melodic mutation.

Fix Toggles (Lock Icons)

Each Euclidean parameter (Steps, Pulses, Rotation, Mutation) has a lock toggle. When unlocked, that parameter itself drifts over time — the pattern length, density, rotation, and mutation rate all evolve. Lock what you want stable, unlock what you want to breathe.

Polyphonic Mode — Strands & Pitch Field

GEN runs up to five parallel Strands (Strand 1 is always active; Strands 2–5 enable individually). Each strand owns its own Euclidean pattern (steps, pulses, rotation, mutation, fix flags), an octave shift, a tempo multiplier, and a textural Role. All strands run the same internal pipeline as Strand 1 (Euclidean rhythm → Turing-machine degree walk → quantization), differing only in their parameters and their pitch source: Strand 1 quantizes to the global Scale, Strands 2–5 draw from the shared Pitch Field. Four secondary strands plus the reference voice means each of the four roles (Anchor / Line / Density / Gesture) can be assigned to its own strand alongside Strand 1, without having to drop one. Inter-strand interaction is governed by the Coordination Mode (see below) — not by per-event harmonic checks inside each strand.

The result is closer to a small ensemble than to a chord arpeggiator: rhythms cross-relate, registers separate, and the pitch material drifts as a whole.

The result is an evolving three-voice texture that never settles — useful for ambient beds, generative practice, or as a melodic source for the diffusion model when paired with Drift/Regenerate.

10. Arpeggiator

The arpeggiator sits after both the step sequencer and the generative sequencer — the chain is always: Sequencer → Arp → Synth Engine. It processes whatever note input it receives, whether from manual steps, GEN mode, or live MIDI. With the generative sequencer, this means an already evolving pattern gets further expanded by the arpeggiator — the combination can produce complex, self-developing textures.

12. Strategies for Finding Sweet Spots

T5ynth is not a sweet-spot synth. Unlike subtractive synths where most parameter combinations produce usable sound, T5ynth's generation process is exploratory and unpredictable. Many combinations produce nothing interesting. That's by design — the instrument rewards patient exploration, not preset-surfing.

1. Start with the source

Get a generation you like before touching synth parameters. A great filter envelope cannot save a bad generation. Experiment with prompts, seeds, and short durations (1–3s) to quickly iterate.

2. Use low step counts for speed

While exploring, keep Steps low to iterate quickly. With Stable Audio Open Small, 8 steps is already the ceiling — more steps add nothing. With Stable Audio Open 1.0, start at 20 and only go higher once you've found a direction worth refining.

3. Exploit the seed

Same prompt + different seed = very different audio. If a prompt feels right but the result doesn't, change the seed before changing the words. Generate 5–10 seeds before concluding a prompt doesn't work.

4. Alpha for morphing, not mixing

Alpha is not a volume crossfade. It interpolates in embedding space — the midpoint (0.0) is often not a blend of A and B but something new. Use Alpha to discover unexpected sounds between your two prompts. Push it past ±1.0 to extrapolate. If you leave one prompt field empty, Alpha sweeps from your prompt through the model-neutral midpoint to its antipode — the Echoraum trajectory, sounds the prompt actively isn't.

5. One axis at a time

Semantic axes are most effective used individually. Stacking three axes at extreme values dilutes each one. Start with one axis and find its useful range before adding a second.

6. Wavetable mode for pitched material

If you need stable pitch tracking (melodic lines, chords), switch to Wavetable mode. Sampler mode preserves the original character but pitch-stretching can introduce artifacts at extreme intervals.

7. Short loops + Scan modulation

Generate 1–2s of audio, switch to Wavetable, and modulate Scan with a slow LFO or Env1. This creates evolving timbres from a single generation — the classic wavetable synthesis approach, but with AI-generated waves.

8. Drift for evolution, not randomness

Set Drift LFO rates very low (0.01–0.05 Hz) and depths moderate (0.2–0.5). Target Alpha or an Axis. Enable Auto-regen. This activates the asynchronous feedback loop — the synth slowly transforms itself, producing a continuous stream of new generations. Far more interesting than random Noise injection.

9. Gen Seq + Arp as a discovery engine

Turn on the generative sequencer with moderate mutation (0.3–0.5), pick a scale, and let it run. Add the arpeggiator in Up mode at 1/16. The combination reveals how your sound behaves across pitch ranges and rhythmic contexts — a rapid audition tool.

10. Save early, save often

Presets include the generated audio, so you never lose a good generation. Export a preset the moment you find something promising. You can always refine the synth parameters later — but the generation itself is hard to reproduce without the exact seed and settings.

Part II

13. How Semantic Axes Work

T5ynth's semantic axes are directions in the 768-dimensional embedding space of the T5 text encoder. Here's how they are constructed:

Step 1: Define Poles

Each axis is defined by two single-word prompts representing opposing concepts — for example, "noise" and "music". These are not arbitrary labels; they were selected through systematic testing of which word-pairs produce the most audibly distinct results.

Step 2: Encode Poles + Neutral

Three embeddings are computed via the T5 encoder:

  • emb_pole_a — encoding of the negative pole (e.g., "noise")
  • emb_pole_b — encoding of the positive pole (e.g., "music")
  • emb_neutral — encoding of an empty prompt ("")

Step 3: Compute Direction

The axis direction is the difference between a pole and the neutral point:

  • Positive values: direction = emb_pole_b − emb_neutral
  • Negative values: direction = emb_pole_a − emb_neutral

This direction vector is then scaled by the slider value and added to the current manipulated embedding.

Step 4: Validation

Not all conceptual oppositions produce audible differences. The 8 axes included in T5ynth were validated by generating audio at each pole and measuring Mel-spectrogram cosine distance. Only axes with a distance above 0.70 at 1-second duration survived. PCA-based axes (derived from variance in the embedding space rather than semantic opposition) were tested but found to collapse at short durations — they produce near-identical audio regardless of slider position.

In the code, axis direction vectors are summed onto the embedding. But because the diffusion model processes the resulting embedding non-linearly, the audible effect is not simply additive — multiple axes are interrelated and can interfere with each other unpredictably. There is no orthogonalization, and axes may partially overlap in embedding space.

14. The DimExplorer In Depth

The DimExplorer (see section 3 for basic usage) operates on the mean-pooled T5 encoder output — the 768-dimensional vector obtained by averaging across the token sequence dimension, weighted by the attention mask. This is the same representation the diffusion model uses for conditioning.

Why Sorted by Magnitude?

Sorting the 768 bars by |emb_A[i] − emb_B[i]| is not just a display convenience — it creates two distinct editing zones:

  • Left side (high magnitude) — dimensions where A and B differ most. Editing here affects what distinguishes your two prompts from each other.
  • Right side (low magnitude) — dimensions where A and B are nearly identical, i.e., what they have in common. Editing here tends to produce more fundamental changes to the overall character of the sound, since you are altering the shared foundation rather than the A/B contrast.

What Does a Dimension Represent?

Individual T5 dimensions do not correspond to human-interpretable audio features. The T5 model was trained on language tasks (not audio), and its internal structure encodes statistical patterns in text — not timbral qualities, pitch, or rhythm. The Stable Audio model learned to map these text patterns to sound, but the mapping is opaque and non-linear. A single dimension might contribute to brightness in one prompt context and to rhythmic density in another. This is why the DimExplorer is a research tool: it rewards experimentation, not prediction.

Technical Details

  • Offsets are applied as manipulated[:, :, idx] += offset_value — broadcast across all tokens in the sequence
  • Only non-zero offsets are transmitted to the inference engine (sparse representation)
  • Offsets persist across generations until explicitly reset
  • The edit history (undo/redo) stores complete offset state snapshots

15. Principles of the Generative Sequencer

The generative sequencer combines three layers of musical algorithm:

Layer 1: Euclidean Rhythm

The rhythmic backbone uses Bjorklund's algorithm (Toussaint, 2005) to distribute N pulses across M steps as evenly as possible. This is the same mathematical principle behind many traditional rhythmic patterns worldwide — it produces patterns that feel natural because they maximize spacing regularity.

Example: 5 pulses in 8 steps → [x . x . x . x x] — a pattern equivalent to Cuban tresillo.

The Rotation parameter shifts this pattern circularly, changing which beat feels like "one."

Layer 2: Stride-Based Melody

Pulses are assigned pitches using a stride permutation through the selected scale. The stride value (max(1, steps − pulses)) determines how many scale degrees to skip between successive notes, chosen to be coprime with the total number of degrees so the melody visits as many pitches as possible before repeating.

The traversal follows a triangle wave (ascending then descending), creating bidirectional melodic motion rather than monotonic climbing. This produces melodies that feel more natural and song-like.

Velocity is derived from the rhythmic gaps — notes after longer rests receive higher velocity, creating natural accents at the start of rhythmic phrases.

Layer 3: Turing Machine Mutation

Each time the pattern completes a cycle, Mutation determines how many notes change. At mutation rate 0.8 with 16 pulses, roughly 8 notes shift by 1–4 scale degrees. The result: the pattern stays recognizable but continuously evolves — like a melodic idea being rethought in real time.

The mutation rate itself breathes sinusoidally over a 16-cycle window, oscillating between 50% and 100% of its set value. This creates alternating periods of stability and flux without any user input.

Parameter Drift

When the Fix toggles are unlocked, the Euclidean parameters themselves drift over 8-cycle windows:

  • Rotation shifts circularly (timing determined by a secondary Euclidean distribution)
  • Pulses increase or decrease over time, changing pattern density
  • Steps expand or contract while preserving the pulse-to-step ratio

All drift is bounded and reversible — parameters oscillate rather than running away. The combination of melodic mutation, mutation breathing, and parameter drift produces patterns that can evolve for minutes without becoming monotonous or losing coherence.

Layer 4: Polyphonic Strands

Up to five Strands run in parallel, each with its own Euclidean DNA, drift state, role, and tempo multiplier. Strands are not voices in a chord — they are independent agents sharing one pitch space. The polyrhythmic interplay arises naturally from the Div× multipliers (1/4×, 1/2×, 1×, 2×, 4× plus 1/3× and 3× for ternary cross-rhythms), which create non-coincident downbeats; cycle boundaries only realign on the lowest common multiple. Each strand runs the same internal pipeline as Strand 1 (Euclidean rhythm + Turing degree-walk + quantization to its register); the role choice only sets default character. How strands interact (or don't) is decided by the Coordination Mode; see §9. With four secondary strands available, every role (Anchor, Line, Density, Gesture) can be assigned uniquely if desired.

Each strand's Role selects a different note-picking strategy from the shared field:

  • Anchor — low-register support. Walks the field via its degree pattern; Dominance probabilistically snaps the note to centerPc at the top of every other cycle (cadential anchor, not a per-beat tic).
  • Line — mid-register voice. Walks the field from its degree pattern.
  • Density — mid-register, shorter gate. On weak steps may take a single chromatic step toward the next field-member landing; the strong-step note remains a field member.
  • Gesture — wider register. Sparse pulses on its own Euclidean pattern.

The Dominance parameter (per strand) controls how strongly the strand returns to centerPc at cycle-pair boundaries. At dominance 0 the strand follows its degree walk freely; at 1.0 the cycle-start note becomes centerPc. There is no per-event harmonic check between strands — that responsibility is moved to the Coordination Mode.

Layer 5: The Pitch Field

All strands draw from a single shared Pitch Field — a 12-bit pitch-class set (pcSet) plus an ordered row used by Transform mode. The field replaces classical chord/scale context with something more flexible: an evolving collection of allowed pitch classes that can transform through several distinct algorithmic paradigms.

The field evolves on Strand 1's cycle boundary at the chosen Rate (1–32 cycles between changes). Four modes:

  • Static — derived from Scale Root + Scale Type, never changes. Use when you want the harmonic field stable while only rhythm and mutation evolve.
  • Drift — each tick, one pitch class is removed and one chromatic pitch class is added (the center pc is protected from removal). Slow harmonic motion that stays close to the starting set.
  • Transform — applies a random twelve-tone row operation each tick: transposition (Tn), inversion (In), retrograde (R), or retrograde-inversion (RI). The new pcSet is then derived from the transformed row's first rowSize members.
  • Pivot — transposes the entire pc-set by the configured Pivot interval (m3, M3, Tritone, P5) each tick, accumulating shift over time. After enough cycles the field returns to its origin (e.g. four cycles at m3 = full octave).

Because all strands share the same field, harmonic coherence is provided at the field level alone: a Drift swap or Transform tick changes what every strand can play. There is no per-event harmonic check between strands — that question is delegated to the Coordination Mode (§9), which currently caps simultaneous voices via priority displacement (DensityBudget). The listener perceives one evolving texture because the strands draw from the same evolving pitch set, not because each note is negotiated against the others.

Roles, dominance, and field modes operate without a Bass/Lead/Pad hierarchy or chord-root tracking. The intent is to produce evolving textures independent of functional harmony. For diatonic results, set Field Mode = Static, Dominance high, and one strand to Anchor.

Key observations from this flow:

  • Prompts, Alpha, Axes, Magnitude, Noise, and DimExplorer offsets all operate before diffusion — they shape the embedding that guides generation.
  • Filter, envelopes, LFOs, effects operate after generation — they are traditional synth processing on the generated audio.
  • Drift LFOs bridge both worlds — they can modulate pre-generation parameters (Alpha, Axes, Noise, Magnitude) and post-generation parameters (Filter, Scan).
  • The Python inference subprocess stays alive between generations — no startup overhead.
  • Determinism is guaranteed across platforms (same seed + settings = identical audio on Mac, Linux, GPU, or CPU) via a patched numpy PCG64 random number generator.

Cutoff Warp Filter — design inspiration

Reference: Surge XT src/common/dsp/filters/CutoffWarp.cpp  ·  License of reference: GNU GPLv3
URL: github.com/surge-synthesizer/surge.

The Warp filter algorithm (src/dsp/CutoffWarpFilter.h) is an original implementation inspired by the Cutoff Warp filter pioneered in Surge XT. The ZDF 4-pole ladder topology is a standard digital filter formulation and not copyrightable in itself; the specific idea of a style-switchable per-feedback nonlinearity as a musical character parameter is credited to the Surge XT project. No Surge XT source code was copied — our implementation is written from scratch against the published ZDF ladder derivation, and our saturation set (Tanh / SoftClip / OJD-like / Sin-fold / Digital / Asym) is a musically curated subset rather than a verbatim reproduction of Surge's full style list.
